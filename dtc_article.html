How sameness, bad AI, and the platform tax killed the direct promise.

Rob Pearson

We do not have a marketing problem. We have a brand problem masquerading as a CAC problem. And the brands still operating from the 2020 playbook are on the clock.

Summary
  • DTC did not fail because the model was wrong; brands treated distribution as strategy and ceded experience to broken AI and ad platforms.
  • CAC inflation and platform fees are structural, not cyclical — the 2020 Meta-only playbook is on the clock.
  • Indistinguishable brands, identical Shopify themes, and chatbots that frustrate more than they convert are symptoms of the same problem.
  • The reset requires brand differentiation, margin discipline, and teams accountable to revenue — not more performance marketing theater.

Summarized with AI assistance and reviewed by the author.

For the past decade, the direct-to-consumer playbook was treated as inevitability. Build a single product and wrap it in a clean Shopify theme, then run Meta ads and capture email, ideally sell more. Raise. Repeat. Investors funded it. Founders worshipped it. And every keynote speaker at every commerce conference pretended it was a religion rather than an arbitrage.

I have spent the last ten years helping brands run this exact playbook. I have watched it work. And over the last eighteen months I have watched it stop working. This is what I am seeing on the ground.

DTC did not collapse because the model was bad. It collapsed because operators confused a distribution mechanism for a brand strategy, ceded the customer experience to chatbots that do not work, and surrendered margin to ad platforms that have no incentive to give it back. The result is a generation of indistinguishable brands selling indistinguishable products to consumers who do not remember any of them, on websites that all look the same, served by AI agents that frustrate more than they help.

This is not a cyclical downturn. It is a structural reset. And anyone still operating from the 2020 playbook is on the clock.

The numbers do not care about your mission statement

eMarketer’s May 2025 forecast pegged US direct-to-consumer ecommerce at roughly 19% of total US retail ecommerce by 2025. They project that share to stay flat through 2028. The category is no longer growing as a share of digital retail. That alone is a damning verdict on a model that was sold to investors as exponential.

Drill into the unit economics. It gets worse. Median DTC brands now spend $130 to $156 to acquire a single customer in 2026. That is roughly a 60% increase over the past five years. Triple Whale’s 2025 benchmark data found that every single industry on Meta saw its CPM increase year-over-year, with the platform-wide median up 20.03%. Health and wellness, once a CAC bargain, saw a 38.03% jump. Google did not help. Wordstream and Mobiloud benchmarks showed Google CPCs up 12.88% year-over-year in 2025, with Shopping CPCs jumping 33.72% to $3.49. Overall ROAS dropped 10.03%.

You are paying more for less. That is not a marketing problem. That is the platforms doing exactly what publicly traded ad platforms do. They are optimizing their take rate.

Meanwhile, the funding spigot closed. Venture investment into DTC dropped 97% from 2021 to 2023, according to data tracked across multiple consumer-tech analyses. The brands that survived had to learn how to operate on cash flow rather than rounds. Median EBITDA margins for eight-figure brands settled at 7 to 8%. Average annual revenue growth for DTC stores dropped to 10% in 2024, the slowest in five years.

This is not a soft patch. This is what the asymptote looks like. Every brand operator who hit the wall in 2024 is now facing the same question. Do we keep paying the platform tax? Or do we rebuild from the ground up?

Sameness is the real tax

Here is the hot take that nobody in the DTC industrial complex wants to admit. The platform tax is not the worst thing that happened to direct-to-consumer brands. The sameness tax is.

Walk through any DTC vertical. Mattresses. Eyewear. Sneakers. Meal kits. Supplements. Skincare. Candles. Dog food. Men’s grooming. Cookware. Picture the websites. They all look the same. Hero image with one overconfident model. Floating product card. Eight customer review stars. Bland product description that sounds exactly like every other brand in the category. Same checkout. Same email capture. Same 10% off pop-up. Same SMS prompt.

This is not an accident. It is the natural output of an ecosystem where the platform (Shopify), the templates (Theme Store), the agencies (one of fifty boutique shops using the same Figma kits), and the tooling (Klaviyo, Yotpo, Postscript, Recharge, the same five Meta-recommended creator agencies) all converge toward a mean. Generative AI did not cause this. It accelerated it. When everyone uses the same tools to write the same copy, run the same retargeting, and ship products from the same three Chinese contract manufacturers, the only differentiation left is price.

A brand without association is not a brand. It is a SKU on a scroll.

Brands earn pricing power because they earn association. Nike does not compete with House of Hoops. Patagonia does not compete with North Face performance fleece. Their customers do not shop them. Their customers belong to them. The sameness economy produces the opposite. Brands without association, customers without loyalty, products without memory. And when memory is gone, marketing has to work harder every quarter to acquire the same person all over again.

That is what the CAC chart is actually measuring. Not the platforms getting greedier. The brands getting weaker.

The performance marketing death spiral

Look at who is winning the math. Meta is. On April 29, 2026, Meta reported Q1 2026 ad revenue of $55.02 billion, up 33% year-over-year. Ad impressions delivered across the family of apps rose 19% YoY. Average price per ad rose another 12% YoY, on top of every increase that came before. Net income hit $26.77 billion, up 61%. Operating margin sat at 41%. Capital expenditure guidance for 2026 was raised to $125 to $145 billion, the bulk of which is going to AI infrastructure that advertisers are effectively financing through CPM inflation. For ecommerce brands specifically, Meta now commands 68.31% of total advertising budgets, more than Google, TikTok, and every other channel combined.

Translation. Meta sold more inventory, charged more for it, and posted a 61% profit jump while the average DTC operator paying those bills watched ROAS continue to fall.

Then Temu and Shein walked in. Between them, those two companies spent an estimated $2.7 billion on digital advertising in 2023 alone, with Temu reportedly funneling $1.2 billion into Meta. Every American DTC brand has been bidding against the deepest-pocketed consumer-arbitrage operations on earth, in a closed auction, where the only counter-strategy is to spend more.

Our team ran the experiment in real client accounts. Cut Meta spend by 30%, revenue dropped 4%. Cut by 50%, revenue dropped 9%. Most of what brands are paying for at the top of the auction is not incremental. It is rent. Reallocating those wasted marketing dollars can move profitability and scale in a way the spend itself never did.

Meta’s pixel-era targeting is permanently degraded, which means the auction is less efficient at the same time it has gotten more expensive. Every dollar buys less signal and less precision.

Stack the inputs. Higher CPMs. Lower opt-in rates. Lower ROAS. Higher return rates. The average ecommerce return rate is 20.5%, fashion runs 20% to 30%, and January post-holiday returns spike as high as 44.5%. Globally, consumers returned over $181 billion in online purchases from the November-to-December 2025 holiday period alone, a 10% year-over-year increase. Returns are not a footnote. For a brand carrying a 60 to 70% gross margin, returns can compress contribution margin by 8 to 15 percentage points before any other variable cost enters the equation.

This is the math. This is why DTC brands keep raising. Not because growth-at-all-costs is a strategy, but because the unit economics no longer survive on their own.

The AI customer service catastrophe

While the math was breaking, the customer experience was getting worse. The biggest unforced error of the DTC era was the wholesale replacement of human customer service with first-generation AI chatbots that do not actually solve problems.

The data on this is crushing. SurveyMonkey’s 2025 customer-experience study found that just 8% of consumers prefer AI over humans for customer service. Kinsta’s research showed that 41% of consumers feel customer service has gotten worse because of AI, and 81% believe AI is being deployed to save the company money rather than to help them. 84% of consumers believe humans are more accurate than AI agents. 63% do not believe AI could ever fully replace humans in service. The Qualtrics 2026 Customer Experience Trends Report found that nearly one in five consumers who used AI for customer service got no benefit at all, a failure rate roughly four times higher than AI use in general.

Every brand operator I work with has a deflection rate dashboard. Almost none of them have a resolution rate dashboard. That is the entire problem in one sentence.

Klarna ran the experiment in public. The fintech famously announced in 2024 that its AI assistant had replaced the work of 700 human customer service agents. By 2025, Klarna was rehiring humans because the AI delivered “lower-quality performance” on complex tasks. The cost-saving math worked on a spreadsheet and broke in the wild.

Here is the underlying problem. Most DTC chatbots are not deployed to resolve. They are deployed to deflect. To bury the contact form. To loop the customer through FAQs the customer has already read. To buy the brand a few more days before the chargeback hits. The CEO of Decagon, one of the better-funded conversational-AI startups, acknowledged in CNBC reporting that the customer-side perception of “deflection” is real even when companies claim to optimize for resolution.

Rip the band-aid off. If your CAC is up 60% and your retention is dropping because customers cannot resolve their issues, you do not have an AI strategy problem. You have a brand-permission problem. Customers used to extend grace to brands they trusted. They do not extend grace to a logo they bought once on TikTok.

Brand equity was the moat. We filled it with discounts.

Step back. The original DTC promise was that by going direct, brands could own the customer relationship, capture more margin, build community, and reinvest into the experience. What actually happened is the opposite. Brands ceded the relationship to platforms, ceded the margin to ad auctions, ceded the community to creators they do not own, and reinvested into discounts.

This is the real reckoning. Not that DTC failed, but that DTC scaled the wrong things. Scale the discount, lose the brand. Scale the ad spend, lose the moat. Scale the chatbot, lose the customer. The economic incentive structure inside Shopify-native DTC pushes operators toward all three of those decisions, every quarter, until there is nothing left to differentiate.

Compare that to brands that resisted the playbook. Patagonia. Levi’s. Lululemon. Even, more recently, On Running and Hoka. These are companies that built association first, then leveraged digital. Their CAC is lower. Their pricing power is higher. Their margins compound. They do not need to discount because their customers do not need a reason to choose them. They already chose.

That is what brand equity actually does. And that is what the past decade of DTC operators traded away for a slightly better Q3.

The body count

Look at the named casualties. Each one was funded, celebrated, and held up as the future of consumer commerce. Each one is now a case study in what the model cannot survive.

Down ~91% from IPO

Allbirds

Went public in November 2021 on the back of a sustainable-materials story and a category-defining sneaker. By April 2024, the stock had fallen below $1 for 30 consecutive trading days, triggering a Nasdaq delisting warning. Q1 2024 revenue dropped 28% year-over-year. Full-year 2024 revenue was guided down to roughly $190 million, a 25% decline. The company closed 10 to 15 underperforming US stores. The product was real. The category was real. The brand could not compete in a sea of indistinguishable sustainable sneakers, including ones from incumbents with bigger distribution and lower CAC.

Down ~95% from peak

Peloton

Became a $50 billion company at its peak. Has lost approximately 95% of that peak market value. The stock peaked at $167.42 in January 2021 and has traded near $4 throughout much of the past two years. Annual revenue went from $4 billion in fiscal 2021 to roughly $2.49 billion in fiscal 2025, a contraction in every successive year. Connected fitness subscribers dropped from 6.4 million members to 6 million between fiscal 2024 and fiscal 2025. The company has cut more than 25% of its workforce across multiple rounds. Peloton is the cautionary tale of a brand that confused pandemic-era demand for permanent product-market fit and built a cost structure that could not survive a return to gyms.

76% writedown in six years

Bonobos

Acquired by Walmart for $310 million in 2017. In 2023, Walmart sold it for $75 million, a 76% writedown. The brand was the last survivor of Walmart’s 2016 to 2017 DTC acquisition spree, which also included ModCloth, Moosejaw, Shoes.com, and Bare Necessities. Walmart wrote off the entire experiment because the DTC customer did not migrate to the parent retailer, and the parent retailer’s customer did not migrate to the DTC brand. Two audiences, two acquisition costs, no synergy.

Stake fell from $130M to $27M

Honest Company

IPO’d in May 2021 at $16 per share, jumped 44% on day one, and within a year had collapsed to $4.52. Founder Jessica Alba’s stake fell from roughly $130 million at IPO to approximately $27 million. The brand had a massive household-name founder, an authentic clean-products story, and a real consumer base. None of it was enough to defend the unit economics against private-label competition from Amazon Basics, Costco’s Kirkland, and Target’s Up & Up.

Down ~72% from IPO

Warby Parker

Warby is the polite cautionary tale, because the brand still exists and has even posted some recent recovery. But its market cap is down roughly 72% from its 2021 IPO, despite running one of the better-executed omnichannel DTC playbooks in the category. If Warby is the success story of this cohort, that should tell you something about the broader basket.

The point is not that any of these companies were poorly run. Several were extremely well run. The point is that even excellent execution against a structurally compromised model produces the same outcome. Capital destruction.

The counter-argument, briefly

Steel-manning the bull case is fair. There are real counter-arguments worth taking seriously.

DTC is not dead, the bulls argue. It is maturing. Total US DTC ecommerce is projected to hit $250 billion in 2026, a 16.6% increase over 2025. AI is making campaign creation cheaper and faster. First-party data is more valuable than ever as third-party signal degrades. Gen Z buys directly from brands at more than twice the rate of the general population, with 28% reporting regular DTC purchases compared to 13% of the total population. Owned channels like email still produce $36 in revenue for every $1 spent, the highest ROI in marketing. The category has plenty of life left. The brands that survive will be leaner, omnichannel, and AI-native. They will print money.

I agree with the second half of that argument. Lean, omnichannel, AI-native brands will print money. I disagree with the first half. The brands that get there will not be Shopify-native DTC operators using the 2020 playbook. They will be operators who treat the website as a brand asset, not a transaction layer. Who use AI to deepen relationships, not deflect them. Who treat paid acquisition as an input, not an identity. Who build association before they build audience. The math only works for the operators who refuse the playbook everyone else followed.

What has to change now

Here is what the next decade looks like, and what we are advising the brands and investors who work with Rareview.

First, brand investment is no longer optional

The era of “we will build the brand later” is over because there is no later. The brands with no association are dying first. The brands with association are surviving, and a few are thriving. Investors evaluating consumer companies should be asking, before they ask about CAC, what is the unaided brand recall in the target customer segment. If the answer is below 10%, the unit economics will not save you.

Second, the sameness trap has to break

Templates, theme kits, AI copy, and beige modern voice cannot be the answer for brands that need to stand out. Distinctive design, distinctive voice, distinctive product point of view, distinctive packaging. None of this is expensive in 2026. It is mostly a function of taste and conviction. Most operators have neither, and they will lose to operators who do.

Third, AI in customer service has to be rebuilt around resolution, not deflection

The current generation of bots is a brand liability. The next generation, deployed correctly, can be a brand asset. The litmus test is simple. Does the AI shorten the customer’s path to outcome, or extend it? If the latter, kill it.

Fourth, the platform tax has to be diversified

Brands cannot keep funneling 68% of their ad budget into one company that has no incentive to make the auction cheaper. SEO, generative-search optimization, retail media, owned content, influencer partnerships, retail distribution, B2B channels. All of these need real budget. Not for diversification’s sake. For survival’s sake.

Fifth, profitability is the new growth

Investors should be skeptical of any consumer-brand pitch that does not show a path to positive contribution margin within 18 months. Growth-at-all-costs is not a strategy. It is a slow liquidation.

The DTC era did not fail. The DTC playbook failed. The model still works for operators who use it as an instrument rather than as an identity. The capital destruction we have watched over the past five years, from Allbirds to Peloton to Bonobos to Honest, is not a cautionary tale about ecommerce. It is a cautionary tale about what happens when an entire generation of brands lets distribution choices substitute for strategic ones.

At Rareview, we work with consumer brands that have stopped optimizing for the playbook that broke. We are rebuilding their growth engines around brand association, margin discipline, and profitability that survives a CAC shock. The metric is not spend and revenue. It is durable, intentional growth.

If you are an investor reading this, the next decade in consumer is going to reward brand discipline, channel diversity, and operational rigor. Not the next iteration of the same Shopify template with a different color palette. Not the next AI chatbot promising to replace the contact form. Not the next 10% off pop-up.

The brands that win from here are not going to be the loudest. They are going to be the ones with the strongest associations and the most patient capital. That is a much harder game than the one we played for the last ten years. It is also the only one left.
